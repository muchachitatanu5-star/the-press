Why this exists

I wanted to go to Bread Loaf. I couldn't afford it.

I'd been writing seriously for years — submitting to journals, losing sleep over line breaks, reading everything I could get my hands on, trying to close the gap between the writer I was and the one I knew I could be. And then I'd look up the workshops. Tin House. Kenyon. Bread Loaf. The ones where real writers went to become more real. $2,500. $3,000. Application required. Acceptance not guaranteed.

There's a particular kind of grief in wanting something that was never designed to include you. Not a door slammed in your face — just a door that was never meant for you to find.

I kept writing anyway. I submitted anyway. I built my own curriculum from craft essays and close readings and the marginal notes of writers I admired. And slowly, something started to work. Not because I found a shortcut — but because I found the right questions to ask of my own work.

The Press is those questions, organized. It's the workshop I built for myself, made available to anyone who can't wait for permission, can't afford the price of admission, and won't stop writing either way.

You don't need anyone's permission to become a better writer. You just need the right set of tools.

— Tania M., creator of The Press
